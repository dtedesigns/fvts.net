Is Christianity Intellectual Suicide?

Haven't modernity and the sciences disproved just about every Christian doctrine? Why, then, does the religion persist? Does it attract the dimwitted and the intellectually lazy? Or does it make people so, breeding ignorance as it spreads?
Join us, but do not check your brain at the door, as we consider if there is any philosophical warrant for Christian belief anymore.

Our Speaker

Nicholas Piotrowski

Nicholas is a resident of Batavia and a PhD candidate at Wheaton College. His dissertation addresses first century socio-rhetorical uses of sacred texts for the sake of community formation. This upcoming talk and discussion will revolve around only a slightly different topic.

Why do we still have communities that adhere to these same sacred texts 1900 years later? Nicholas has noticed that the common answer is that religious people—and Christians in particular—are simply bird-brains. Whether it’s the sort of people ABC News interviews, the commentators on MSNBC, or the characters in popular stories, the lament (and irritation) is common: “Christians have been left behind by modernity, science, politics, and art,” and need to “leave the cry baby stage [of life] and finally come of age.” Such a pervasive sentiment caused Nicholas to think: are all Christians really such dunderheads? Is becoming a Christian tantamount to checking your brain at the proverbial door? And is the religion itself to blame? Does Christianity attract the slow, the uneducated, the unreflective, the unenlightened, and the intellectually lazy? Or does it make people so?

In short, is Christianity intellectual suicide? Before moving to the Fox Valley Nicholas taught, among other things, History and Philosophy at the Washington Christian Academy in Olney, Maryland. There he was able to flesh out and develop many of his burgeoning ideas on this topic, in the context no less of a rigorous college-prep high school. On October 23rd he’ll briefly share some of his thoughts before opening the floor to question and dialogue.
